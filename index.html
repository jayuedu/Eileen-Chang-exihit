01

Theoretical Framework

Four intersecting traditions that ground the analytical framework

This project situates Eileen Chang's fiction at the intersection of four theoretical traditions, each of which contributes a distinct analytical lens. Together, they allow the study to move beyond impressionistic literary judgement towards a falsifiable, empirically grounded argument.

Meng Yue & Dai Jinhua · 1993

1940s Chinese Feminist Literary Theory

In Emerging from the Horizon of History, Meng and Dai characterise the dominant mode of 1940s Chinese female literature as positioning women as passive victims of the feudal patriarchal system. Eileen Chang represents a marked deviation: her female characters are neither victims nor heroic resistors, but pragmatic "accomplices" and "survivors" who navigate the patriarchal structure through calculated material exchange.

Rita Felski & bell hooks · Contemporary

Agency & Sisterhood Hypothesis

Contemporary feminist theory foregrounds female agency and cross-class sisterhood as normative ideals. hooks argues that female mutual aid formed beyond individual interests constitutes the ethical core of feminism. This project treats this proposition as a falsifiable hypothesis rather than a theoretical given — using network density and discourse strategy data to test empirically whether such unconditional sisterhood exists within Zhang's textual world.

Daphne Spain · Gendered Spaces

Spatial Feminism

Spain's feminist geography holds that the physical division of space reinforces social power hierarchies. This project extends Spain's insight to Eileen Chang's micro-domestic settings, arguing that the mansion, the corridor, and the medicine shop are not neutral backdrops but active determinants of discursive possibility. The analytical focus therefore shifts from macro-geographical mobility to micro-spatial topology.

Sara Mills · Feminist Stylistics

Discourse & Power

Mills argues that discourse dominance — measured through utterance length, frequency, and strategic function — directly maps to a character's position in the social power structure. This underpins the project's dialogue act classification system. The three-category scheme of Plot Triggering, Passive Response, and Information-revealing operationalises Mills' framework in a computationally tractable form.

The central tension this project exploits is the gap between the idealism of contemporary feminist theory (sisterhood, agency, spatial freedom) and the pragmatism of Eileen Chang's colonial women. By quantifying this gap through computational methods, the project moves from literary intuition to empirical evidence.

02

Methodology

Three-dimensional DH approach: dialogue classification · spatial topology · network analysis
01

Data Extraction & Annotation

218 dialogue turns from Aloeswood Incense and 170 from Chained Links were extracted and annotated. Each turn was classified by speaker, characters present, spatial node, and discourse strategy label using a GPT-4 few-shot prompt implemented in VS Code. A 10% human-in-the-loop validation sample was reviewed for annotation quality.

02

Spatial Categorisation

Individual spatial nodes were aggregated into three functional categories per protagonist. For Ge Weilong: Patriarchal Core (Study, Bathroom), Transitional (Corridor, Steps, Garden), and Semi-Autonomous (Guest Room, Exterior). For Ni Xi: Elevated Residence (Upstairs Room, Villa), Labour/Commercial (Medicine Shop, Kitchen), and Liminal/Transit (Convent, Mountain Road).

03

Network Construction & Visualisation

Directed social network graphs were constructed using dialogue co-occurrence: an edge from speaker A to character B whenever A spoke and B was present. Edge weight encodes interaction frequency; edge colour encodes dominant discourse strategy. Networks were prototyped in Gephi and rendered as D3.js visualisations. Quantitative charts were produced using Plotly.

Dialogue Act Labels

LabelDefinition
Plot Triggering (PT)Utterances that initiate new narrative events, introduce information that changes plot trajectory, or establish new relational dynamics.
Passive Response (PR)Reactive utterances that answer questions, comply with requests, or affirm the interlocutor's agenda without introducing new propositional content.
Information-revealing (IR)Utterances that transmit factual information or background context at the request or direction of another character.

Two-Novel Contrastive Design

Rather than treating two novels as a small and insufficient sample, the project frames them as a paired contrastive case: two texts sharing a colonial Hong Kong setting and the macro-spatial ascent plot structure, but operating through different mechanisms of suppression.

F-F Networks · Sisterhood Hypothesis

In both novels, no F-F edge is PT-dominant. Ge Weilong–Liang Taitai (n=71): IR 46%. Ni Xi–Melainie (n=35): PR 60%. Female-to-female interactions function as channels of information control or passive subordination — not emotional mutual aid.

F-M Networks · Power Gradient

Ni Xi–Yahya: PR 67% (n=93). Ni Xi–Mr Milner: PR 81% (n=27). But Ni Xi–Thomson: PT 65% (n=17). Discursive agency is a relational variable calibrated to the power differential of each interlocutor.

05

Conclusion

Answers to the research questions · theoretical implications · limitations

This project set out to determine whether spatial position systematically determines discursive power for female characters in Eileen Chang's colonial fiction. The evidence presented across four analytical layers consistently supports an affirmative answer. Female dialogue in both texts is predominantly characterised by Passive Response or Information-revealing rather than Plot Triggering, and this distribution is not random but spatially structured.

The higher the degree of patriarchal surveillance associated with a given space, the lower the protagonist's discursive agency within it. F-F interactions, far from instantiating the "sisterhood" posited by contemporary feminist theory, function as mechanisms of information control and passive subordination, with senior female figures serving as active agents of patriarchal surveillance rather than as allies.

Key Findings
→
In Aloeswood Incense, Ge Weilong is subjected to functional confinement: her speech volume is not suppressed, but its function is locked into information-transmission (IR 74.5% in core spaces), stripping her of narrative initiative regardless of location.
→
In Chained Links, Ni Xi is subjected to selective suppression: a 24-percentage-point gap in Plot Triggering between elevated residence spaces (23.5%) and labour spaces (47.9%) demonstrates that spatial type — not personal character — determines discursive agency.
→
F-F interaction networks in both texts are IR- or PR-dominant, providing computational evidence against the idealised sisterhood hypothesis in this colonial-patriarchal context.
→
The F-M power gradient — with PR rates of 67–81% toward highest-status males, falling to PT rates of 65–77% toward lower-status males — confirms that discursive agency is a relational variable calibrated to power differential, not a fixed character attribute.

The two novels operate through different mechanisms but arrive at the same structural conclusion: macro-spatial ascent does not produce micro-level discursive empowerment. Framed as a contrastive pair rather than a small sample, they mutually reinforce rather than simply replicate each other's evidence.

Limitations & Future Directions
The corpus is restricted to two novellas, limiting generalisability to Eileen Chang's broader oeuvre. Future work should extend to additional texts including The Golden Cangue and Love in a Fallen City.
The spatial categorisation scheme involves interpretive decisions that introduce a degree of subjectivity, particularly for scenes set in ambiguously coded spaces.
LLM annotation may not fully capture Eileen Chang's ironic register, where surface-level passivity may conceal strategic intent — a case for a dedicated "performative agency" label in future iterations.
Cross-authorial comparison with contemporaries (e.g. Su Qing, Guan Lu) would test whether the mechanisms identified are specific to Eileen Chang or characteristic of colonial female writing more broadly.
